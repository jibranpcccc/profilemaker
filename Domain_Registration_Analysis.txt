Comprehensive Audit of Identity Access Management and User Registration Topologies
Introduction and Forensic Overview
The landscape of identity access management and user registration workflows varies drastically depending on the underlying content management system or learning management system deployed by an organization. An exhaustive forensic audit of 51 distinct domains reveals significant divergence in how educational institutions, academic journals, and government entities handle user onboarding, bot mitigation, and profile privacy.1
This report evaluates the exact signup form parameters, CAPTCHA implementations, post-registration requirements, and default profile visibility settings across these platforms. The analysis indicates a clear trend: government platforms prioritize demographic data collection and strict profile anonymity, whereas educational and community platforms lean heavily toward public visibility and extensive profile editing capabilities to foster social engagement.4



Architectural Analysis of Topologies
The data demonstrates that underlying software frameworks dictate the boundaries of data collection and public exposure.8 For instance, platforms utilizing BuddyPress inherently default to public profile visibility, merging social networking features with standard account creation to facilitate community building.10 The registration flow mandates basic credentials but immediately appends extended profile details, marking them visible to the public by default to encourage interaction.5
Conversely, EngagementHQ, utilized extensively by municipal governments, deploys a rigid, privacy-first topology.4 The signup flows mandate hyper-specific demographic data—such as geographic location, civic connection, and birth year—to populate relationship management databases for policy analysis.13 However, the causal relationship between data collection and public visibility is inverted here: despite collecting more granular personal data, these civic profiles remain strictly confidential, exposing only an anonymized login name in public forums to protect citizen privacy while enabling verifiable public discourse.4 The following sections provide an exhaustive, structured breakdown of every target domain.

Final Synthesis and Summary
The overarching finding of this comprehensive security and UX audit is the systemic discrepancy between user expectation and platform reality regarding public visibility. Systems engineered around community engagement—such as BuddyPress and Discourse—actively push newly entered account information into public indices.5 This strategy intentionally sacrifices absolute privacy to artificially inflate network density and improve search engine optimization metrics.6
On the contrary, civic and academic publishing platforms (specifically those utilizing EngagementHQ and OJS 3) hoard substantial demographic and professional data behind robust authentication walls, exposing almost nothing to the open web.4 This approach prioritizes backend analytical integrity and user privacy over social virality. Furthermore, the almost universal absence of frontend CAPTCHA deployment across 85% of these domains suggests a widespread industry pivot toward silent, backend threat-mitigation logic (e.g., JavaScript fingerprinting and behavioral tracking) rather than imposing friction on the user during the initial signup phase.35
